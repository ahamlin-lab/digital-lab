7  Chapter 7: Joints (Articulations)

7.1 Objectives

At the conclusion of this lab, you should be able to:

  • Define a joint (articulation).
  • Classify joints by structure and by function.
  • Identify and describe fibrous, cartilaginous, and synovial joints.
  • Identify the components of a synovial joint.
  • Describe and demonstrate common joint movements.
  • Identify examples of each joint type in the human body.

7.2 Prelab Activity 7.1 – Definitions

7.2.1 General Joint Terms

  • Articulation (joint) – site where two or more bones meet
  • Ligament – connective tissue that connects bone to bone
  • Tendon – connective tissue that connects muscle to bone
  • Bursa – fluid‑filled sac that reduces friction around joints
  • Meniscus – fibrocartilage pad that improves fit between bones

7.2.2 Structural Classification of Joints

  • Fibrous joints – bones joined by dense fibrous connective tissue; no joint cavity
  • Cartilaginous joints – bones joined by cartilage; no joint cavity
  • Synovial joints – bones separated by a fluid‑filled joint cavity

7.2.3 Functional Classification of Joints

  • Synarthrosis – immovable joint
  • Amphiarthrosis – slightly movable joint
  • Diarthrosis – freely movable joint

7.3 Types of Joints

7.3.1 Fibrous Joints

  • Suture – immovable joint between skull bones
  • Syndesmosis – bones joined by a ligament (e.g., tibia–fibula)
  • Gomphosis – tooth anchored in alveolus

7.3.2 Cartilaginous Joints

  • Synchondrosis – joint united by hyaline cartilage (epiphyseal plate)
  • Symphysis – joint united by fibrocartilage (pubic symphysis)

7.3.3 Synovial Joints

  • Plane (gliding)
  • Hinge
  • Pivot
  • Condyloid (ellipsoid)
  • Saddle
  • Ball‑and‑socket

7.4 Prelab Activity 7.2 – Synovial Joint Structure

7.4.1 Components of a Synovial Joint

Structure Function
Articular cartilage Reduces friction
Synovial cavity Contains synovial fluid
Synovial fluid Lubricates and nourishes cartilage
Joint capsule Encloses the joint
Fibrous capsule Provides strength
Synovial membrane Produces synovial fluid
Ligaments Stabilize joint
Bursa Reduces friction
Meniscus Improves joint fit

7.7 Joint Movements

7.7.1 Angular Movements

  • Flexion – decreases joint angle
  • Extension – increases joint angle
  • Hyperextension – extension beyond normal
  • Abduction – movement away from midline
  • Adduction – movement toward midline
  • Circumduction – circular movement

7.7.2 Rotation and Special Movements

  • Rotation – bone turns around its axis
  • Supination – palm facing anteriorly
  • Pronation – palm facing posteriorly
  • Dorsiflexion – toes move upward
  • Plantar flexion – toes point downward
  • Inversion – sole turns inward
  • Eversion – sole turns outward
  • Elevation – movement superiorly
  • Depression – movement inferiorly

7.9 Post Lab Activity 7.1 – Concept Questions

  • What is a joint?
    A joint is a site where two or more bones meet.

  • Which joints are freely movable?
    Synovial joints.

  • Which joint type allows the greatest range of motion?
    Ball‑and‑socket joint.

7.11 Post Lab Activity 7.3 – Fill in the Blanks

  • The three structural classifications of joints are fibrous, cartilaginous, and synovial.
  • Freely movable joints are called diarthroses.
  • Synovial fluid is produced by the synovial membrane.
  • Ligaments connect bone to bone.

7.12 Summary

  • Joints allow movement and stability.
  • Synovial joints are the most complex and movable.
  • Joint structure determines joint function.
  • Proper joint anatomy is essential for locomotion and posture.
